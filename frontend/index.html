Portfolio Theory — How to Diversify

Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT)

Harry Markowitz (1952): combine assets that don't move together to get the same return at lower risk. The "efficient frontier" is the set of portfolios with the best risk/return trade-off. Key insight: diversification is the only free lunch in investing.

The 55 / 30 / 15 Rule (Your Policy)

55% Large Cap · 30% Mid Cap · 15% Small Cap. Large caps provide stability and liquidity. Mid caps offer growth with moderate risk. Small caps add return potential but increase volatility — keep them capped.

Sector Diversification

No single sector >15% of portfolio. Sectors move in different phases of the economic cycle. Technology/financials often lead; utilities/healthcare are defensive. Indian equity benefits from overweighting domestic consumption (FMCG, Pharma) over pure cyclicals.

Stock Concentration

Maximum 8% in any single stock. With 15–18 stocks you get ~90% of the diversification benefit of an index. Beyond 20-25 stocks, incremental diversification is minimal — you're just tracking the index at higher cost and complexity.

Sharia Constraints

Sharia screening naturally excludes financials (banks, insurance) and some cyclicals — this creates a tilt toward tech, healthcare, industrials, and consumer goods. This tilt has historically outperformed in high-growth markets. The main gap: financials are ~30% of Indian indices, so a Sharia portfolio will look very different from Nifty.

Rebalancing Trigger

Rebalance when any stock hits 12%+ of portfolio or any sector hits 20%+. Do it by deploying new money first (add to underweight) before selling — minimises churn and tax events. Review quarterly.
